Chapter 1

The Players & Their Utility Functions

In game theory, each player has a utility function — a ranking of outcomes by preference. Understanding what each actor maximises is the foundation of the entire analysis. These are not abstract figures; each preference is grounded in real statements and behaviour.

🇺🇸
Trump / United States
Dominant Player · Rational Transactionalist

Trump is a coercive dealmaker. His strategy is maximum pressure → offer deal. He does not seek permanent war; he seeks a televised victory monetisable diplomatically and economically (Hormuz oil flows, market stability).

Utility Ranking (Best → Worst)
  • Denuclearised Iran + open Hormuz + US controls "nuclear dust"
  • Regime change → pro-US government (Venezuela model)
  • Managed ceasefire with face-saving terms
  • Prolonged war → market chaos + US casualties
  • Ground invasion quagmire (worst case)
"We want the nuclear dust. We're going to want that, and I think we're going to get that." — Trump, March 2026
🇮🇷
Iran / IRGC
Survival-Maximiser · Asymmetric Resister

Iran's utility is regime survival above all. Its nuclear programme is an existential deterrence card. Enriched uranium is both leverage and shield. The new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei is unseen; the IRGC may be the true decision-maker now.

Utility Ranking (Best → Worst)
  • Ceasefire with sovereignty preserved + sanctions lifted
  • Prolonged resistance → US war fatigue → negotiated exit
  • Hormuz as leverage chip in negotiations
  • Nuclear capability traded for full sanctions relief
  • Regime collapse / occupied Tehran (catastrophic)
"Iran is negotiating with itself." — Iranian officials dismissing Trump's claims, March 2026
🇮🇱
Netanyahu / Israel
Maximalist · Regime-Change Hawk

Netanyahu has dual utility: strategic (eliminate Iranian threat) and personal (ICC warrant, corruption charges, political survival). A premature deal that leaves Iran in place looks like failure to 93% of Israelis who support regime change. He is a veto player on any ceasefire Trump offers.

Utility Ranking (Best → Worst)
  • Regime collapse + Reza Pahlavi / allied government in Tehran
  • Iran militarily castrated — "mowing the grass" indefinitely
  • US-brokered deal that strips Iran of nukes + proxies
  • Premature ceasefire that lets Iran reconstitute
  • Trump abandons war → Iran survives intact
"You can't make a revolution from the air — there must be a ground component." — Netanyahu, March 2026
🇵🇰
Pakistan
Mediator · Trapped Neighbour · Strategic Broker

Pakistan shares a 900km border with Iran, gets 90% of its oil through Hormuz, has a large Shia population, and has spent the past year cultivating Trump's trust. It is simultaneously Iran's neighbour and America's preferred messenger. A ground invasion would force Islamabad to choose sides — a choice with no good answer.

Utility Ranking (Best → Worst)
  • Successful mediation → diplomatic capital + Hormuz reopens
  • War ends before ground invasion → Pakistan uninvolved
  • Limited role as message-relay channel (current position)
  • Ground invasion + Pakistan forced to grant basing rights
  • Full escalation → sectarian spillover across the border
"Pakistan stands ready and honoured to be the host for meaningful and conclusive talks." — PM Shehbaz Sharif, March 2026

The Payoff Matrices

We construct three 2×2 payoff matrices — the core analytical tool of game theory. Payoffs are ordinal: 4 = best, 1 = worst. ★ Nash Equilibria are marked.

Matrix 1: United States vs. Iran — The Core Prisoner's Dilemma
Iran strategies: Negotiate vs Resist. US strategies: Deal vs Escalate. Payoffs: (US, Iran).
US ↓ / Iran →IRAN: NegotiateIRAN: Resist / Deny Talks
US: Offer Deal (3, 3)Pareto Optimal — mutual best if trust exists (1, 4)Iran gets leverage; US looks weak
US: Escalate (4, 1)US wins militarily; Iran capitulates ★ (2, 2)Nash Equilibrium — mutual hurt, neither deviates unilaterally

⚠ The Nash Equilibrium (Escalate, Resist) = (2,2) is Pareto Inefficient. Both players are worse off than (Deal, Negotiate)=(3,3). Iran's fear of 2018-style JCPOA betrayal and US fear of Iran stalling drive both into this inferior equilibrium. Rational actors, irrational collective outcome.

Matrix 2: Trump vs. Netanyahu — The Alliance Game
The war-within-the-war. Trump wants a deal; Netanyahu wants escalation. Payoffs: (Trump, Netanyahu).
Trump ↓ / Bibi →BIBI: Support CeasefireBIBI: Continue Strikes
TRUMP: Push Deal (4, 2)Trump wins; Netanyahu accepts reduced goal (1, 4)Netanyahu sabotages Trump's deal narrative
TRUMP: Back Escalation (2, 3)Israel weakens Iran; Trump accepts delayed deal ★ (3, 4)Nash Equilibrium — Netanyahu dominates; Trump backs it to maintain alliance

⚠ Netanyahu has a strictly dominant strategy: Continue Strikes regardless of Trump's move (4 > 3, and 4 > 2). Trump's best response is to Back Escalation. This explains why the war persists even as Trump claims diplomacy is working.

Matrix 3: Pakistan's Two-Front Dilemma
Pakistan must simultaneously manage relationships with the US and Iran. Payoffs: (Pak-US score, Pak-Iran score).

⚠ Pakistan's dominant strategy is Neutral Message-Relay — it maximises expected utility across scenarios. Pakistan only deviates to Full Mediation if it believes a deal is genuinely achievable. Currently, that belief is fragile.

Chapter 4

The Extensive Form: How This Game Actually Plays Out

The extensive-form game tree shows the sequential nature of decisions. We solve by backward induction — reasoning from the end of the tree to the beginning to find the subgame perfect equilibrium.

Backward Induction Result: Iran's dominant strategy at Move 3 nodes is to deny talks and resist. Netanyahu's dominant strategy at Move 2 is to obstruct any deal. Therefore, Trump at Move 1 faces: if he offers a Deal, it will be obstructed (payoff 1). If he Escalates, he gets payoff 2–3. Backward induction predicts escalation as the equilibrium path, even though a negotiated deal (payoff 3,3) is Pareto superior for both Trump and Iran.

Chapter 8

Why the Prisoner's Dilemma Cannot Self-Resolve

The game-theoretic reason this conflict persists is not irrationality — both Trump and Iran are behaving rationally given their constraints. The problem is structural. Three interlocking failures prevent the Pareto-superior outcome from being reached.

01
The Commitment Problem

Iran cannot trust any US commitment. Trump withdrew from JCPOA in 2018. Iran gave nuclear concessions; the US gave nothing in return. This is a renegotiation-proof contract problem: any deal Iran signs can be unilaterally revoked by the next president. Without third-party enforcement, Iran's rational strategy is to reject any deal requiring irreversible nuclear concessions upfront.

EVIDENCE: "Iran's deepened mistrust" after US joined Israel's surprise attack Feb 28, 2026 — even as Oman declared talks "within reach."
02
The Veto Player Problem

Netanyahu is a veto player in the US-Iran game. Even if Trump and Iran reach a deal, Netanyahu can undermine it by continuing strikes. CNN analysts noted: once Trump "decides this campaign is over, I don't think Israel can continue it" — BUT Netanyahu has demonstrated he will escalate until that moment, making stable agreements nearly impossible.

EVIDENCE: Netanyahu says "more to come" even as Trump announces 5-day pause (March 25, 2026). Classic spoiler behaviour in GT terms.
03
The Authority Problem

Who speaks for Iran? The new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei has not been publicly seen since being named. Trump says he spoke to a "respected" Iranian leader (possibly Qalibaf); Qalibaf denies it. Even if back-channel deals are struck, the IRGC may not honour what the foreign ministry agreed. Iran's internal game is unresolved.

EVIDENCE: "It's not certain anyone entering talks with the US would be speaking on behalf of the regime." — AP, March 2026.

THE GAME-THEORETIC VERDICT: This conflict is locked in a subgame perfect Nash Equilibrium of attrition. Both the US and Iran are behaving rationally given their information and constraints — but their rational choices produce a collectively irrational outcome (war) versus the Pareto-superior alternative (negotiated deal). The three structural barriers — the Commitment Problem, the Veto Player (Netanyahu), and the Authority Problem — make self-resolution without external shock unlikely.

What breaks this equilibrium? Three possible shocks: (1) Iran's Hormuz closure causes so much US economic pain that Trump overrides Netanyahu and forces a deal — changing payoffs. (2) Iran's internal coordination game resolves — a clear authority emerges willing to sign — solving the Authority Problem. (3) A major US military setback changes Trump's cost calculus — updating beliefs. Until one of these shocks occurs, the game predicts a prolonged, attritional stalemate punctuated by periodic diplomatic signals — exactly what we are seeing in March 2026.

Pakistan's endgame: Pakistan achieves its best equilibrium payoff only if a ceasefire is reached before a ground invasion. The 5-day pause Trump granted is Pakistan's window. If talks fail, Pakistan enters a strictly dominated outcome regardless of what it chooses — a trapped mediator in a war that was never its to control.
